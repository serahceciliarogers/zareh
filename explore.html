Zari/Zardosi is an elaborate and luxurious embroidery technique, historically
characterized by its use of genuine gold and silver threads, complemented by beads
and spangles, meticulously applied to rich fabrics such as velvets, brocades, and
silks. The term "Zardosi" itself originates from Persian, signifying "embroidering with
gold threads."


The historical decline of Zari under rulers like Aurangzeb was partly attributable to
the prohibitive cost and scarcity of pure raw materials. The modern adoption of
imitation zari directly addresses this historical barrier, making the craft more
affordable and widely available. While this adaptation has been crucial for the craft's
survival and broader market appeal, it introduces complexities regarding the
perceived "luxury" and intrinsic value of the product. This duality can affect artisan
compensation and the long-term preservation of the traditional "true zari"
technique, presenting a challenge for effective branding and market positioning.
